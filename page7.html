Early Spanish Colonial Institutions

Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade was considered to be the economic lifeline of the colony in the 16th century. The Galleon Trade route allowed Spain to trade with Asia via Mexico. It brought exotic goods like porcelain, silk, and spices from China to Mexico (Hecht, 2003).

These goods would be exchanged for silver. The first galleon successfully returned to Manila in 1565, and the last galleon from Acapulco arrived in Manila on September 14, 1815, which ended the 250-year-long trade (Naholowa‚Äôa, 2022).

In Spanish colonies, the Encomienda system rewarded conquistadors, soldiers, and officials with forced labor from the Indios in exchange for military protection and evangelization. This was mainly done to extract more wealth from the colonies and convert more people to Christianity (Cartwright, 2022).

The third objective of the Spanish was evangelization. To achieve this, they created settlements called pueblos where they can sustain the population and land (Gomez, 1998).
